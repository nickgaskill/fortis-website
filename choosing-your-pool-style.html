Geometric, freeform, or custom?

How to choose the pool style that fits your home, your yard, and the way you'll actually use it.

Every pool project starts with the same decision: the shape of the water. It sounds cosmetic, but your pool's style drives almost everything downstream — the decking, the coping, the landscape around it, even which finishes look right. Here's how we walk Austin homeowners through it.

Geometric: let the house lead

Geometric pools — rectangles, squared corners, clean 90-degree lines — borrow their language from architecture. If your home has a modern or transitional look, a geometric pool reads like it was always part of the plan. They're also the natural choice for lap swimmers and for tighter city lots, where a crisp rectangle uses every foot efficiently.

Modern freeform: let the land lead

Freeform pools trade corners for curves. Done well — and that qualifier matters — a modern freeform pool looks like it grew out of the landscape instead of being dropped onto it. It's the right call for hill-country lots with trees, slopes, and views, where a rigid rectangle would fight the terrain. The soft edges also pair beautifully with natural stone hardscape.

Completely custom: let the vision lead

Some projects don't fit either box — a vanishing edge over a canyon view, a swim-up bar off the outdoor kitchen, a tanning ledge shaped around a heritage oak. Custom means the design starts from a blank page and your wish list. It costs more and takes longer, and for the right yard it's worth every bit of both.

Three questions that settle it

Who's using it most? Kids cannonballing, adults doing laps, and friends floating on a Saturday point to three different pools. What does your house look like? The pool should agree with the architecture, not argue with it. What does the lot want? Flat and open favors geometry; sloped, treed, or irregular favors freeform or custom.
